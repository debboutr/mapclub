Maps from the Article

Map 1

Michael Mercator’s 1595 map of the western hemisphere has two C. Blancos in what is now Brazil, by the mouth of a small river called Arboledas up the coast from the Amazon and north of Recife [actually somewhat off the mark from C. Branco, which is near Recife]; one C. Blanco in present day Argentina south of the mouth of the Rio de la Plata; and one in Costa Rica. Michael Mercator was a very serious young man. Map 2

Theodor De Bry’s 1596 map of the western hemisphere has four imaginary C. Blancos on the west coast and a mysterious C. Blanco in Labrador


Map 3

Denis Diderot issued a side-by-side comparison in 1772 of two older maps of the northwest coast [just as non-natives were beginning to really encounter the coast] by a cartographer named Vissher in 1612 and 1641, respectively without and with imaginary C. Blancos.


Map 4

Cornelius de Jode’s stunning and imaginary 1593 map of the northwest coast perpetuates a typical Plancius-style array of four imaginary C. Blancos.


Map 5

Perus Kaerius’ 1607 remake of the seminal Plancius 1590 world map has the four typical imaginary C. Blancos and one in Mauritania, like Plancius’ map, but none in Rio de la Plata. The Kaerius map also has a C. Blanco in Labrador, possibly copying de Bry, and a mysterious C. Branco [Portuguese] in Tierra del Fuego.


Map 6

Abraham Ortelius’ 1606 map of the western hemisphere has C, Blancos in Costa Rica, Rio de la Plata, and Arboledas. It also marks a cape on the Bay of Saint George [Argentina] called C. de Tres Puntas, which is usually known as C. Blanco. Most specially, the Ortelius map includes the earliest mapping I know of C. Blancos at two far flung tips of the Americas, Chile and Newfoundland. From the fortieth parallel in the south to the fiftieth parallel in the north, one quarter of the earth’ circumference. That’s a nice big slice of planet bracketed by C. Blancos!


Map 7

Jocodus Hondius, in his 1606 map of the western hemisphere, ties with Ortelius for earliest Newfoundland C. Cabo mapping. Hondius has the Costa Rican C. Blanco labeled under its other name, Farallon. Hondius marks both Argentinian C. Bancos, at the Bay of Saint George and the Rio de la Plata. Most especially, this map by Hondius is the earliest I know of marking a C. Blanco in the mouth of the Amazon.
